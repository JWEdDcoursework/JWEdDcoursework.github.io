5 Instructional Strategies
for Higher Education
📚
Book Talks
Students share and discuss texts with peers, connecting outside sources to course learning and collaborative knowledge building. Excellent for graduate seminars with extensive reading lists, this strategy provides opportunities for students to explore specialized topics while learning from diverse perspectives and scholarly interests within their cohort.
💭
Think-Pair-Share
Three-step collaborative process: individual reflection, paired discussion, then group sharing for active learning. This cooperative learning approach promotes knowledge construction while serving as a low-stakes assessment tool. Students gain new perspectives on topics while reflecting on their own understanding and developing critical thinking skills through structured peer interaction.
🏃‍♂️
Four Sides
Kinesthetic learning where students physically move to corners representing their agreement levels with statements. Particularly effective in social sciences like education, sociology, and political science, this strategy encourages reserved students to participate non-verbally while helping all students visualize the spectrum of opinions and positions within their peer group on complex issues.
⚡
Mini Lessons
Short 5-15 minute focused lessons that scaffold complex topics or preview upcoming content for better comprehension. These targeted instruction bursts can teach specific skills, establish context, or generate interest in larger topics. Excellent for breaking down challenging material into manageable chunks and can be strategically repeated throughout units to reinforce understanding at key moments.
🏗️
Scaffolding
Gradual release model guiding students from instructor-led practice to independent mastery step-by-step. This approach helps students learn new processes or improve existing methods through progressive responsibility transfer. The instructor models each step multiple times, demonstrating best practices while students gradually take on larger roles until achieving full independence in executing complex tasks.
